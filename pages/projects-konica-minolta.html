UX design in the built environment

Project Architect / Project Manager, WK Design Group

The sensitivities of a designer aren't limited to one platform; I discovered that I was a UX designer while working in the domain of architecture.

To demonstrate some of the parallels, I'll step through a couple of interior design decisions for the Konica-Minolta Business Innovation Center (BIC) expansion. As the interior designer and project architect / manager, a large part of my work centered on stakeholders' and end-users' goals.

Studies of human behavior, movement, physiology, and biology are central to all kinds of design, from town planning to an LED blinking pattern.

UX designers and architects listen and observe

Built environments affect human experience. While UX design is relatively new to software, architects have been practicing human-centered design for centuries.

Architects meet with stakeholders and end-users for months, usually weekly, to converge on a design to meet clients' goals. This requires empathy. To prioritize goals, we attune ourselves to clients' needs (e.g. workflows, maintenance, intended use). We respect branding, budgets, and schedules. Architects also synthesize engineers' and builders' expertise, and jurisdictional requirements, to make sure a design can be realistically achieved.

UX designers and architects are systems thinkers

Architects work at multiple scales simultaneously, designing new systems within and adjacent to existing systems: usage patterns, customs, processes; logical and safe circulation; federal, state, and local requirements; building standards and engineered systems integration; repeatable components or modules for efficiency.

Large scale systems: The purpose of this diagram is to “identify and quantify the factors of design that impact the human experience.” Itended for architecture, it could easily apply to UX design. Credit: Gensler Research Institute 2017 Experience Index

Small scale systems: Design guidelines that I handed off for installation, covering multiple conditions that would be encountered during implementation.

UX designers and architects communicate design intent for implementation

We communicate intent, not rigid requirements, allowing other experts on the team to problem-solve. Artifacts facilitate discussion: sketches, representations, visual layouts, mappings in 2, 3, and 4 dimensions. Each communication is a mini-UX problem: for end-users to exchange feedback, for stakeholders to justify the project to management, for construction managers to align to budget and schedule, for engineers to calculate, for authorities to approve, for contractors to build.

(Left) For stakeholders & end-users: one of many “samples” I used to communicate initial concepts. (Right) For general contractors: "Pricing Plans" and "Finish Schedules" assign quantitiy and quality guidelines to the desired concept, for rough baseline pricing. Design adjustments based on rough pricing reveal how the client prioritizes quality, cost, and schedule.

For vendor & installer hand-off (implementation): after we adjusted the design to balance quality, cost, and schedule, I moved forward with a full carpet and furniture layout.

For installer hand-off (implementation): design systems often include special accents that break from the system. Detail drawings highlight those instances; in this case, the hexagonal tiles bordering a customer-facing area.

Tangible Results

The stakeholders at Konica-Minolta appreciated the playfulness and sohpistication that the carpet design brought to the space without blowing the budget. They requested that it be added to their global design guidelines for future offices worldwide.
