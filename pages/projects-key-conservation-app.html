Key Conservation:

Onboarding designs for Key Conservation as part of a two month capstone project.

Why Key Conservation Matters

Biodiversity ensures the resilience of habitats – including human habitats. In the past, its care fell to an expert community of scientific professionals through research, policy, and fieldwork.

Key Conservation enables that expert community of conservationists to enlist any smartphone-carrying individual to infill gaps in support: from awareness of local surroundings, to equipment lending, to active volunteering of time and skills to address urgent initiatives. It empowers average individuals to affect change, and to track the effects of their specific contributions – no PhD required.

Why Focus on Onboarding?

The Key mobile app built a community that was recruited from two groups: Conservation organizations, and individual supporters – connecting experts and enthusiasts for a shared cause.

Organizations made up the backbone of the Key community. The app was structured such that their active involvement furnished the content through which supporters could engage and contribute.

The pre-existing onboarding process deterred the organizations needed to draw in supporters. A majority of motivated "pilot" organizations had stalled during the onboarding process. Less motivated proxy end-users who we interviewed stated that they were likely to abandon it prior to completion.

Our Challenge: Redesign the app's onboarding flow for organizations – for improved follow-through, and new memberships.

Scoping & Research

Given an aggressive timeline, and limited access to both Key Conservation staff and applicant organizations, we customized our approach to research:

  • Reviewed the pre-existing onboarding process
  • Uncovered stakeholders' goals
  • Compared onboarding flows from adjacent application-domains

Reviewing the Pre-existing Process

Screenshots of desktop-only website, application form, airtable survey, pdf instructions, in-app profile form, and app screens.

The screenshots above show a lengthy pre-existing process that spanned multiple devices: it entailed a fifteen-question form, a document upload, twenty-two detailed survey questions, a pdf how-to guide email attachment, a form with twelve inputs required to populate a profile page, and two approval wait times.

By diagramming the existing onboarding process, we exposed breaks in the user flow.

By diagramming the sequence of screens that the end-user encountered, above, we could easily spot the hurdles that had likely contributed to drop-out:

  • Two multiple-day waits for approvals (red clock icons)
  • Five different environments to navigate between (horizontal bands)
  • Environments optimized for mobile-only and desktop-only viewing (device icons)

A fundamental problem with the pre-existing approach: it assumed an abundance of applicants from whom to select; however, without the outreach focus to recruit hundreds of applicants, only eleven pilot organizations had joined, after years of product planning.

A more welcoming, guided approach could entice applicants, encourage completion of membership, and engage members to stick around.

Uncovering Stakeholders' Goals

We identified the key stakeholders for organization onboarding as Key Conservation (the client) and applicant organizations (the end-users).

Through interviews with the director of Key Conservation, we identified primary business objectives for onboarding:

  • Build trust: Screen applicant organizations to ensure legitimacy so that supporters don't have to.
  • Engage: Encourage active participation of organizations to attract supporters.
  • Excite: Facilitate a process that builds applicant enthusiasm about potential gains from joining.

In parallel, we walked through the pre-existing onboarding process with proxy end-users – representing organization account administrators – and asked them to "think out loud." The exercise revealed that end-users had a different set of primary objectives for onboarding:

  • Align purposes: Be assured that Key Conservation is a serious organization with a clear mission.
  • Offer value: Understand time and energy required vs. value received, prior to commitment; beyond the value offered by more established apps.
  • Make it easy: A painless path to begin participating.

Comparative Investigation of Other Onboarding Flows

We researched strategies used by other social-benefit mission-based apps, and apps in which an end-user group must be screened. AirBnB and various financial apps relied on a solid vetting process. World Wildlife Fund was well-known for motivating end-users to make donations. AirBnB Open Homes encompassed, at a high level, all of the challenge characteristics as Key Conservation.

Our Biggest Challenge

We learned early-on that our client was passionately attached to the existing app design and processes – because they were completely her creation. The client had imagined the features, designed the marketing materials, and had even drawn the artboards for the mobile screens.

In order to advocate for the end-user for a successful product outcome, the UX team had to thread the needle on multiple fronts:

To accommodate, or to challenge? The client's assumption was that visually stimulating graphics would solve the applicant follow-through problem. As UX designers, we evaluated follow-through at a fundamental level, and our research exposed structural issues that cosmetic adjustments would not fix.

To add, or to subtract? The client's proposed solution was additive – spice up a boring process by using pre-selected dynamic backgrounds, on-trend graphics, and effects. The UX team's findings suggested a subtractive approach – simplify the process and visuals to help the end-user move quickly over the administrative hurdle.

Wings, or roots? The client's ideas were big-picture and visionary. Our appraisal of her ideas were detailed and evidence-based.

Improved Onboarding Designs

Some interfaces best serve end-users by inviting them to linger, while other interfaces are meant to be moved through with ease. The onboarding process fit the latter category, combining function and aesthetics to roll out the red carpet to member organizations.

A Structurally Improved User Flow

Primary objective: Encourage follow-through of a lengthy onboarding process, while providing Key Conservation with business data from member organizations.

At the center of our onboarding solution was a commitment to clarity. We simplified the information architecture, thereby smoothing out the user flow for potential member organizations.

Proposed: An improved user flow would simplify a complex onboarding process and consolidate it to one environment.

In the improved user flow, we:

  • Removed three environments and one waiting period entirely.
  • Created a parallel track wherein the user can opt to complete the profile while waiting for approval.
  • Moved the entire process to occur in-app.
  • Recommended that the marketing site and mobile app be made responsive (future scope).
  • Paired each requested input field with the stated intent of each step.
  • Removed almost 40 survey questions that did not map to directly to an onboarding step – saving them for short post-approval in-app surveys.

Secondary Design Wins... & Losses

Secondary Goal: To visually project trust, clarity, and reliability, while respecting the client's brand aesthetic.

Our primary objective was to improve onboarding follow-through. Graphically, we could boost Key Conservation's credibility by refining the design system. Following are three examples of how we challenged the pre-existing visual design.

Projecting Clarity through Typeface Hierarchy

The original typefaces limited hierarchy options and were not mobile-friendly.

The pre-existing typefaces were more suited for desktop headlines than for mobile device content, and their limited weights made text hierarchy a challenge. We proposed two mobile-friendly font families which came in multiple weights: Lato and Open Sans. Since the pre-existing content was so text-heavy, we chose Lato, which was narrower but did not compromise legibility.

After: A simple sans-serif typeface with multiple weights allowed for better readability optimized for mobile environments.

Improving Accessibility by Expanding the Color Palette

The original color palette did not allow for multiple button or field states.

The pre-existing palette contrast ratios only met WCAG 2.0 AA compliance standards in limited combinations. Neon on black appeared off-message. This left few options to depict states and to differentiate primary focus, and no options for secondary focus.

We proposed alternative palettes reflective of conservation themes, but the client felt that the typical conservation aesthetic was dull.

A sampling of proposed alternative color schemes.

The pre-existing color palette was ultimately deemed off-limits, but we negotiated to expand it to allow for accessible combinations.

After: An expanded color palette provided more options for states and affordances.

Supporting Mission & Message by Challenging Brand Imagery

The pre-existing brand graphics included a set of animal skin and fur textures. We were tasked with incorporating them into the onboarding process as a means to engage the applicant.

The marketing imagery promoting the Key App heavily relied on up-close animal textures.

Our first concern was whether the textures were sending the wrong message. We created a simple test in which fifteen participants were each shown one of the mobile screens below, and asked to guess the subject. On the left was the pre-existing splash screen. The middle screen reflected a traditional conservation aesthetic, with a highly emotive image. The right screen was a hybrid – retaining the use of texture, while adding context and emotion by including the animal's eye.

User test results compared the existing loading screen and two alternate approaches to imagery and brand.

Responses for the pre-existing screen showed that participants correlated the image with pets rather than wild species; and apparel rather than live animals. However, the textures were central to the brand (and the client's) identity. Changes were off the table.

Our second concern was about clarity. From our research, it seemed unlikely that follow-through would improve with flashier screens. We proposed keeping form input screens free of clutter, and incorporating textures as backgrounds on timed transition screens. On those screens, we selected the client-provided textures least likely to be associated with pets or apparel.

The timed screens had the added bonus of reinforcing the new structure of onboarding steps, while aligning with the client's bright and bold aesthetic.

What We Delivered

Having wrapped up a critical part of the Key app, we prepared a deliverable package that would set up the next team for success with a smooth transition. This included:

  • The improved user flow diagram
  • Figma artboards and click-through prototype of the organization onboarding process
  • User test and analytics goals for this and future iterations
  • Suggested product roll-out, prioritizing core functions and documenting the Client's full app vision
